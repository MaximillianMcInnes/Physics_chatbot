5 Electricity
Measuring the output voltage with a meter
This can be done using a digital voltmeter with very high (if not virtually infinite) resistance. The output voltage measured by this meter is that across R2, in other words V2.
Another option is to use a moving coil meter. These meters have a much lower resistance than a digital meter, usually some tens of kΩ. This means that the combined resistance of R2 and the moving coil meter in parallel with it is less than R2. The proportion of the input voltage (V)
dropped across R2 therefore falls and so the output voltage is less than that measured with a digital meter.

Examples of the use of a potential divider
Light-dependent resistor (LDR)
An LDR is a component that has a resistance that changes when light falls on it. As the intensity of the light is increased, so the resistance of the LDR falls.
If the LDR is connected as part of a potential divider, as shown in
Figure 5.19, then as the light level is increased its resistance falls and the proportion of the input voltage dropped across it will also fall.

Thermistor
If R2 is replaced by an NTC thermistor the circuit is temperature dependent. As the temperature of the thermistor rises its resistance falls and so the voltage dropped across it falls.
